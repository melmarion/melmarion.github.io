What I actually do

Most developers ship features. I ship behavioral models.

The engineering is a given — 50,000+ lines of Swift, production React, Python backends, Core Graphics rendering where every pixel is calculated, not loaded from a sprite sheet. Every developer has a tech stack. The question is what sits underneath the interface.

ReadTheRoom looks exactly like your iMessage inbox. Same bubbles. Same typing indicator. Same three dots that make your stomach tighten a little while you wait for a response. Except every person in your inbox is fictional, and every conversation is a scenario where real persuasion psychology determines whether you succeed or fail.

14 characters. Each one has an attachment style, Big Five personality scores, defense mechanisms, and specific vulnerabilities. You can send the same message to the anxious-attached friend and the avoidant coworker and watch it land completely differently. You can’t memorize a script. You have to read the person.

I built a scoring engine across four dimensions: read, rapport, frame, close. And then I built the last character — a therapist — and gave her a single entry where her defense mechanisms should be: “None — she detects yours.”

Every influence technique fails on her. The anchoring that worked on character six. The loss framing that cracked character eleven. The strategic empathy that character three never saw coming. None of it works. The only thing that scores points is the one thing you can’t manufacture.

I didn’t design that as a philosophical statement. It emerged from the system. Forty techniques, tested against fourteen personality models, and the conclusion the math arrived at is that authenticity outperforms strategy. That’s not something I decided. The architecture proved it.

When you open Forecast, you see a weather app. Cloud cover. Temperature. Atmospheric pressure. A sky that shifts from clear to overcast to stormy across the day. Only you know what it actually means. The weather is your hormones. The forecast is your cycle. And the reason it’s disguised is a design constraint that drove every pixel: this is nobody’s business but yours.

Underneath the weather metaphor there’s a pattern engine tracking data across months — correlations your conscious mind would never catch. And a Vault for the entries you’d never put in an app anyone could see over your shoulder.

Social Pilot is the one I didn’t expect. It started as a tool to schedule Reddit posts. Then it grew a viral scoring engine. Then a behavioral science content generator. Then an autonomous commenting system with timing enforcement and anti-detection. Then a pattern library backed by 20 peer-reviewed psychological mechanisms. Then a content predictor. Then a dating assistant. Then a cross-domain viral research engine.

56 tools. 85 automated tests. A fine-tuned 7B LLM. It runs while I sleep. It learns which comment styles work in which communities, rewrites its own content strategy every week, and — the part that still surprises me — it knows the difference between a comment that performed well because the writing was good and one that performed well because the thread went viral. It separates context from craft. Most analytics platforms built by funded teams can’t make that distinction. This one does it automatically from a script running on my laptop.

You’re probably starting to notice something about these projects.

They don’t share a tech stack. They share a question: what happens when you treat human behavior as a system that can be modeled, tested, and made visible?

You get products that don’t just serve users. They anticipate them.

And if you’ve been building products for any length of time, you already know — that’s the piece most teams are missing. Not another feature. Not a faster backend. A model of the person using it that’s accurate enough to know what they need before they ask.

Here’s what working with me actually looks like. Your team has a feature that’s technically correct but users aren’t adopting it. I look at the same screen everyone else looked at, but I see something different — the onboarding flow assumes people are rational when they’re actually anxious. The notification timing triggers reactance instead of action. The progress bar moves linearly when your users’ motivation curve is logarithmic. I build the behavioral model underneath, and the feature starts working. Not because the code changed. Because the psychology did.

I’m not sure that fits into a job title. But I know exactly which teams need it. And I suspect you’re reading this because yours is one of them.
